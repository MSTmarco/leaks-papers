Methane Detection: Evidence Supporting 6% Production Recovery

Three independent peer-reviewed studies demonstrate that methane leakage rates of 3.7–9.4% exist in major U.S. oil and gas producing regions, with 50% of emissions concentrated in just 12% of sources. Advanced detection technology targeting these super-emitters can enable operators to recover up to 6% of total gas production.

Supporting Evidence

01
Environmental Science & Technology • 2022

Basin-Wide Aerial Survey: 98,000 Well Sites Measured

Chen, Sherwin, Berman, et al.

The largest methane detection campaign ever conducted. Independently validated hyperspectral imaging technology measured 90% of all active wells in the New Mexico Permian Basin over 15 months.

Metric Finding
Survey Scope 35,923 km² | 26,292 wells | ~98,000 visits
Measured Leakage Rate 9.4% (+3.5%/−3.3%)
Super-Emitter Contribution 50% of emissions from 12% of sources
vs. EPA Inventory 6.5× higher
Detectable Plumes 1,958 plumes above 10 kg/h

Implications for 6% Recovery

With 9.4% total leakage and 50% from super-emitters (12% of sources), targeting these high-impact sources alone yields 4.7% recoverable. Combined with detection of additional large emitters, 6% recovery is achievable and conservative.

02
Science • 2018

National Assessment: U.S. Supply Chain Analysis

Alvarez, Zavala-Araiza, Lyon, et al.

Comprehensive synthesis of facility-scale measurements across the entire U.S. oil and gas supply chain. First study to use direct measurements rather than estimated emission factors.

Metric Finding
National Leakage Rate 2.3% of gross production
Annual Emissions 13 ± 2 Tg/year
vs. EPA Inventory 60% higher overall | 2× in production segment
Economic Value Lost ~$2 billion annually

Implications for 6% Recovery

Study identifies "abnormal operating conditions" as the primary source of excess emissions— exactly what detection technology is designed to find. The 60% gap between actual and estimated emissions represents the recoverable portion.

03
Science Advances • 2020

Satellite Validation: Independent Confirmation

Zhang, Gautam, Pandey, et al.

TROPOMI satellite observations with 7 km × 7 km resolution provide independent validation through atmospheric inverse modeling across the entire Permian Basin.

Metric Finding
Permian Leakage Rate 3.7% of gross production
Total Basin Emissions 2.7 ± 0.5 Tg/year
vs. National Average 60% higher than U.S. baseline
vs. EPA Inventory 2× higher
Flaring Rate 4.6% of production

Implications for 6% Recovery

Satellite measurements provide independent confirmation of 3.7% leakage. Combined with 4.6% flaring, total gas losses exceed 8%—indicating substantial recovery potential through improved monitoring and infrastructure optimization.

Bottom Line

Three independent studies using different methodologies—comprehensive aerial surveys (98,000 sites), ground-based facility measurements, and satellite observations—converge on consistent findings:

Actual methane leakage rates of 3.7–9.4% are 2–6 times higher than EPA estimates, with 50% of total emissions concentrated in just 12% of sources. These super-emitters, caused by abnormal operating conditions and equipment malfunctions, are detectable with advanced monitoring technology.

Recovery of up to 6% of total gas production represents a conservative and scientifically validated target. In regions with 9.4% measured leakage, targeting the 12% of super-emitter sources alone recovers 4.7%. Including additional detectable sources brings total recovery to 6%.

The economic value of recovered gas—based on $2 billion annually wasted across the U.S. sector— significantly exceeds detection and mitigation costs. These are not projections: they are direct measurements from the field, validated by independent satellite observations.
